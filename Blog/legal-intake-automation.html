How I Reduced a Law Firm's Client Intake from 20 Minutes to 30 Seconds

Every new client email was a 20-minute job. Read the email, extract the details, download the attachments, check if they were already in the system, type everything into the CRM. By hand. Every time. Here's how we fixed that.

// The full intake pipeline - from inbound email to verified case file

When I first spoke to this firm, they weren't sure automation was even relevant to them. They're a small legal practice in Lagos - a tight team, handling a steady stream of client inquiries every week. Not a big operation, but a busy one.

The person I spoke to described their intake process the way most people describe something they've stopped really seeing: "We just handle it manually. Someone reads the email, puts the details into our system, makes sure the files are saved. It's fine."

It wasn't fine. It was just so normal to them that they'd stopped noticing how much time it was taking.

What "Handling It Manually" Actually Looked Like

Here's what happened every time a potential client sent an email to the firm.

Someone on the team would open the email and read through it. If there were attachments - documents, evidence, contracts - they'd download those to their desktop. Then they'd open the firm's case management software (they use Clio) and search for the client's email address to check if they were already in the system. If they were a returning client, they'd create a new matter and link it. If they were new, they'd create a contact first, then create the matter.

Then they'd type a summary of the case into the matter notes. Not a copy-paste - an actual typed summary, because the original email was usually unstructured and they needed it in a specific format. Then they'd go back to the email, download the attachments again if they'd forgotten, and save them to the right folder in Google Drive. If the folder didn't exist yet, they'd create it first.

The whole thing took 15 to 20 minutes per inquiry. On a quiet day with two or three inquiries, that's manageable. On a busy week, that's hours of time from someone qualified enough to be doing actual legal work.

"The emails don't stop coming at 5pm. But the person processing them does."

There was another problem nobody had really named yet: the process only ran during office hours. A client who emailed on Friday evening waited until Monday morning. A client who emailed at midnight before a hearing sat in an unread inbox. The firm had no visibility into those inquiries until someone physically sat down to open their email.

The Actual Cost of This

Let's be straightforward about the numbers. If you're processing 10 client inquiries a week at 20 minutes each, that's over 3 hours of staff time - every week - on a task that produces nothing except a record that should have been created automatically. Over a year, that's roughly 160 hours. That's a month of working days spent copying information from emails into software.

And that's assuming no mistakes. In practice, manual data entry means occasional typos in names and email addresses. It means sometimes a returning client gets created as a new contact because someone didn't search carefully enough. It means a duplicate record in Clio with the case history split across two entries. Cleaning that up takes time too.

// The Hidden Cost

Duplicate client records in a legal CRM aren't just inconvenient. They mean fragmented case history - past matters, notes, and billing records that don't surface when you search the client's name. The error has compounding downstream effects.

What We Built

The goal was straightforward: the moment a client email arrives, everything that used to happen manually should happen automatically. By the time anyone opens Clio, the work should already be done.

STEP 02 // AI Reads the Email and Extracts Structured Data
The full email is passed to GPT-4o-mini with a carefully written prompt. The AI is instructed to return one thing only: a JSON object with six fields. Client name, client email, case type, urgency level, a 2-3 sentence case summary, and whether a follow-up is required. No freeform text, no preamble - just the data. The prompt is specific enough that the output is consistent regardless of how informally the client wrote their original email.
STEP 03 // Data Cleaned and Normalised
A formatter step extracts the case summary as a clean string from the AI's output, and normalises the client's email address into plain text. That email address becomes the identifier that connects every downstream step - the folder name, the Clio search, the document title. Everything traces back to it.
STEP 04 // Case Folder Created in Google Drive
A new folder is created in the firm's Google Drive, named using the pattern [client email] - [date received]. Every case gets the same structure. No inconsistent naming, no folders in the wrong place, no manual filing.
STEP 05 // Attachments Filed Automatically
Any attachments from the original email are uploaded directly into the new case folder. PDFs, Word documents, images - whatever the client sent - moved from the inbox and filed correctly without anyone touching them.
STEP 06 // AI Summary Saved as a Google Doc
The case summary the AI generated gets saved as an actual Google Doc inside the case folder, titled "Case Summary - [client email]." When the attorney opens the folder, they don't have to read the original email thread to understand the situation. The brief is already there.
// Auto-generated case folders in Google Drive - one per client, named consistently
STEP 07 // Clio - Find or Create the Contact
Before anything is created in Clio, the system searches by email address. If the client already exists - a returning client, a previous inquiry - their record is returned and used. If not, a new contact is created. This is the duplicate prevention step. It runs on every single intake, without exception. There's no longer a "sometimes we check and sometimes we don't."
STEP 08 // Clio - Matter Created and Note Logged
A new matter is created in Clio, linked to the contact, assigned to the correct practice area and attorney. The AI-written case summary is logged as a note on the matter, timestamped with the original email's received date - not the time the automation ran. The audit trail is accurate. Open Clio, open the matter, and the intake brief is sitting there exactly when the client first made contact.

The whole pipeline runs in under 30 seconds. By the time anyone at the firm opens their email, Clio already has the contact, the matter, and the case summary. Drive already has a folder with the attachments and an AI-written brief. Nothing is waiting to be done.

The firm no longer has a backlog on Monday mornings from weekend emails. They no longer have duplicate client records to clean up. Attorneys open Clio and the case context is already there - they didn't have to write it, they didn't have to find it. It's just there.

But the result I think matters most isn't in those numbers. It's that the people doing legal work are now doing legal work. Not data entry.

A Few Things Worth Knowing If You're Thinking About This

This system uses OpenAI's API to process the email content. For most practices, that's completely fine - the data sent is no more sensitive than what you'd share with any cloud software. But if your work involves particularly sensitive client matters and you'd prefer not to use a third-party AI, it's possible to run a local language model instead. The output quality is slightly lower but the privacy guarantee is absolute. I've built both versions.

The other thing worth saying: this wasn't a complex build. It took about a week from the first conversation to having it running live. The technology isn't exotic - Zapier, OpenAI, Google Drive, Clio. These are tools that already exist. What takes time is the logic design: figuring out the right extraction prompt so the AI output is consistent, building the search-or-create step correctly so duplicates genuinely can't happen, making sure the timestamps are accurate. That's the work. The tools are just how you execute it.

"The technology isn't the hard part. Working out exactly what needs to happen, and in what order - is where the real work is."

If your firm is handling more than five or six client inquiries a week and they're all being processed manually, the maths are pretty simple. You're spending hours every week on a task that a system can do in seconds. That time is going somewhere, and it's probably not where you'd choose to spend it if you thought about it that way.
